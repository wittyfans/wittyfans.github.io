Week2

Key point speech overview

Lesson Summary

  1. What is the key point speech?
    A speech with main ideas and supporting information
  2. How long is it?
    For this class,3-7 minutes
  3. What do I talk about?
    Something appropriate for general audience that has main points and support

Key points speech rubric

My question?

  • Should I show what that looks done well ,and what it’s done not well?

Lesson Summary

  1. Rubrics are helpful because they:
    • reflect course aims.
    • guide practice
    • sharpen criticism
  2. The key point speech rubric includes all the cannons; invention, arrangement, style memory, and delivery.

This rubric is our map to this course and it’s certainly a general guide to better speaking.

How to?

Invention.

  • included and explained appropriate main points that clarified the speech topic.
  • included and explained appropriate support that clarified the main points.

Arrangement.

  • arranged the speech elements appropriately.
  • orally highlight speech structure clearly and effectively.
  • previewed, transitioned, and reviewed the main points clearly and effectively.

Style.

  • phrased main points clearly and concisely.
  • discuss support clearly and concisely.

Delivery.

  • appeared confident and animated.
  • breathed and projected appropriately and effectively.
  • performed pitch and rate appropriately and effectively.
  • used notes effectively and appropriately.
  • moved and gestured appropriately and effectively.

Speech length (scored 1-3).
The speaker delivered a speech that was:

  • An appropriate length (~3-6 minutes)
  • Too short (1:30-2:45 minutes) or too long (6:15-7:30)
  • Significantly too short (under 1:30) or too long (over 7:30)

A model for memorable points

The components of a memorable point

  • Claims
    • An assertion that you want the audience to take as valid, statement with support or elaboration
  • Support’s
    • Support’s just the evidence and information used to illustrate and validate the claim, And you need explain how and why the support relates to the claim

Lesson Summary

  1. We can define an argument as a claim with warranted support.
  2. Claim: an assertion that you want the audience to take as valid
  3. Support: evidence used to validate your claim
  4. Warrant: what links the support to the claim

(Test)Can you hear these components?

  • as speakers, we can be clearer if we make our claim, support, and warrant, easy for audiences to hear. And as listeners, we can evaluate speeches more insightfully, once we uncover the relevant elements.

Inventing you key points

Inventing key points:Topics

what do you include or exclude from this talk? Don’t include any contend that seem like poor topic. Such as the history of western civilization for five minutes speech.

Lesson Summary

  1. Inventing key points takes time and revision.
  2. Try running your ideas through various patterns.
  3. Maybe start with:
    1. Short term/long term
    2. Past/present/future
    3. Increase/decrease
    4. Cause/effect
    5. Division
    6. Definition

Inventing key points:How many points?

  • Too many points makes us boring.
  • Group points!,9->3

Lesson Summary

  • How to Break up larger presentations depends on you contend.
  1. There is no perfect number of key points for a speech
  2. Try Start with 2-5 key points.
  3. Break up larger presentations into “chunks” of 2-5 key points.

Arranging and phrasing your key points

Arranging key points:Subordination

Subordination usually means order the points helps us better understanding the relationship, help audiences to follow along with. And you should make the points violate the thesis.

Lesson summary

  1. Subordination:All your key points all relate to the topic.
  2. Subordination: your points helps you keep the speech from wandering off on tangents

Arranging key points: Coordination and discreteness

Coordination, they work well together,Discreteness, they don’t overlap with each other. Don’t prepare too big points , they may actually encompasses each other. Every point in a speech didn’t have same size and length.

Bad example
T(Topic):The communication department has a lot of great programs.

  • We have a wide range of excellent undergraduate programs.
  • Our study abroad program is outstanding.

Points is Too big, First point encompasses second.

Revise
T:The communication department has a lot of great programs.

  • We have exciting academic research programs.
  • We have excellent professional development programs
  • And we have a wonderful study abroad program.

No real overlap between these points. they’re covering separate topics.

Bad example
T:I’m a climatologist studying the solar impacts on Arctic ice.

  • What does climatology study?
  • September Arctic sea ice is now declining at a rate of 13% per decade.

this points need more balance. Try talk about your specialty

Revise
T:I’m a climatologist studying the solar impacts on Arctic ice.

  • What does climatology study?
  • What do solar impacts tell us about climate change?
  • why is Arctic ice a good way to study this?

each key point takes us deeper into the topic but in logical measured steps

Lesson Summary

  1. Your points should be coordinate. They should work together.
  2. Your points should be discrete. They shouldn’t don’t overlap… too much.

Stylizing key points: Phrasing

Let’s talk about four strategies for writing good key points.diff language may have diff ways. But the goals are the same. The question is, how can we phrase key points well? Remember ,The phrasing on a key point’s not the entire point. It’s just there to help the listener understand what you will talk about

Lesson Summary

We Want our main points to be phrased memorably and concisely. How can we do this?

  1. Craft short phrases.
  2. Use evocative words.
  3. Put the key terms in important positions.
  4. Use parallel phrasing, if appropriate

For Revise

Let’s try our hand at some arrangement and phrasing. Take a look at this outline.

Thesis: Students should study public speaking at every level of primary education (roughly ages 6-12).

  1. Public speaking is often thought of as a lifelong skill, so it is best to begin that study early and often.

  2. Research indicates that fifth-graders show significant academic and personal benefits from public speaking instruction.

  3. Many schools already do this in the form of reports and show and tell. So, moving to a more formalized system would not prove too onerous for most school districts.

  4. There are some strong benefits of having to articulate one’s understanding of new concepts and public speaking instruction can help structure that system more formally.

Those points are kind of a mess.

Week 2 review

Many times the chunks that we work with in a speech are really just a collection of claim support and warrants. Then we worked through our key points. Now remember, Aristotle’s prompts can help you come up with those two to five key points. And once you’ve got those, you should arrange them in a way that they’re subordinate to the thesis or to the key idea. And they’re coordinate discreet with one another. Then when we get to phrasing, the focus is on phrasing these concisely, but really paying attention to where the key term is in that phrase itself. So, I hope this week has given you some tools to help you better prepare speeches.
