OverView

Goals

By the end of the course, you should have the skills and experience necessary to design and deliver speeches that are clear and compelling.

Assignments

  • Speeches
  • Quizzes
  • Analysis activities

Schedule

  • Week1:Understanding Speech
  • Week2:Making Ideas Compelling and Memorable
  • Week3:Illustrating and Delivering Your Ideas
  • Week4:Good delivery and reducing you fear of speaking
  • Week5:Course Conclusion and Final Speech

Week 3

Overview

This week we’re going to start off working with the speech support. This include find the right support, discussing it well ,and using it to drive those claims. We will talk about introductions, transitions, and conclusion.

Finding the right support

You can have large claims supported by smaller claims. And support will provides more detail.

  1. What does good support do?
    Shows validity and provides details.
  2. Types of support
    1. Facts, statistics, and testimony
    2. Examples and illustrations
    3. Metaphors and analogies

Discussing your examples concisely

When discussing your support:

  1. Focus on the relevant details.
  2. Focus on clarity over comprehensiveness.
  3. Help the audience craft a mental image.

Performing your key point

Don’t just march from the top of your outline to the bottom, Include repetition and amplification.

  1. State: give us the claims.
  2. Explain it: unpack the claim and prime us for the support
  3. Show it: explain the support and how it relates to the claim.
  4. Conclude it: touch on the key claim again as a way of wrapping up.

Highlighting speech structure

You need provide previews in the introduction that let the audience know where we’re going. We can highlight the transitions between the main ideas in our speech. And provide reviews in the conclusion.
Tips:

  • clear speech more better than unclear speech
  • Remember speech is not a mystery,

    Lesson Summary

  1. Help your audiences orient themselves to your content with introductions, transitions, and conclusion.
  2. Practice this arrangement talk so it doesn’t sound too blunt.
  3. Help your audience in this way doesn’t “spoil the ending”
  4. But save this arrangement talk for appropriate speech genres when you need to be clear.

Introductions

Good introcuction needs to let you audience know that your’ve started. For that ,you can ask other for help. Now, once the audience knows that we’ve started, we should give them a sense of what we’re going to be talking about.
Focus on the introductions’s functions.

  1. Open the speech.
  2. Orient the audience.
  3. Provide a preview.

Transitions

  1. Good transitions:

    1. Demonstrate the distinctness of each point
    2. Help the audience know where you are
    3. Give the audience just a little break(use your body language)

  2. Many transitions are both perfofmance and content. The audience should hear and feel a transition.

Conclusion

Conclusion reinforces the key points.and it’s a signal to finish your speech.

Lesson Summary

  1. Conclusions reinforce key points.
  2. Conclusions should provide sense of closure.
  3. Your final line should sound like a final line (usually slower and more deliberate)

Revising your speech

the art of writing is in the rewriting ,and that’s certainly true of speech writing
Generally you prepare top to bottom. come up with the thesis first, and then hammer out some key points, and as you doing so, you drop in the support. But then you revise bottom to top to make sure everything’s working.

Lessons Summary

  1. Revise for fit between claims and support
  2. Look for rearrangement opportunities.
  3. Try a couple of different models.

Practicing and remembering your speech

This episode discuss How to decide what to write out, practice and memorize. Outline allows you to speak more naturally ,and it generally doesn’t take as long to prepare.Do a outline, practice it, but find those words spontaneously, you’ll sound more natural and build in the types of repetition that can help listeners. Manuscripting is more difficult, but it really allows you to dial in the timing exactly. I hate not looking good on stage more than I hate practicing. Not by much, but just enough. And I’ve done thousands of talks.
How to memory speech? You need know memory isn’t a single stage, it should be part of the writing, revising, and practicing.
